Momentum

Learning Objectives

Learners should be able to 

Momentum

  • Define momentum as the product of the mass and velocity of the object
  • State that momentum is a vector
  • Calculate the momentum in one dimension of a moving object using p = m v p=mv

Newton's Second Law Expressed in terms of Momentum

  • State Newton's second law in terms of momentum: The net force acting on an object is equal to the rate of change of momentum. (Note: there are two acceptable statements of Newton's Second Law)
  • Solve problems for constant mass using  F n e t = Δ p Δ t  F_{net}=\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}

Conservation of Momentum and Elastic and Inelastic Collisions

  • Explain that an isolated (or closed) system is one that has no net external force acting on it
  • Explain (when working with isolated systems) what is meant by internal and external forces
  • State the law of conservation of linear momentum: The total linear momentum of an isolated system remains constant (is conserved)
  • Solve problems by applying the law of conservation of momentum to interactions of two objects moving in one dimension (along a straight line) with the aid of an appropriate sign convention
  • Define an elastic collision as a collision in which both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved
  • Define an inelastic collision as a collision in which only momentum is conserved
  • Identify elastic and inelastic collisions using calculations where necessary

Impulse

  • Define impulse as the product of the net force and the contact time  i m p u l s e = F n e t Δ t impulse=F_{net}\Delta t
  • Know that impulse is a vector quantity
  • Know that  F n e t Δ t F_{net}\Delta t is a change in momentum, i.e.  F n e t Δ t = Δ p F_{net}\Delta t = \Delta p
  • Solve problems using  F n e t Δ t = Δ p F_{net}\Delta t = \Delta p
  • Apply the concept of impulse in everyday life, e.g. airbags, catching a hard ball

Introduction

Photos of bicycle, motor car and truck

Consider a bicycle, a motor car and a truck. Which of them is more likely to result in less damage in a collision situation, why? Would you agree that how massive the object is and how fast the object is going will determine the amount of damage.

Newton introduced a quantity of motion which included the mass and the velocity of the object.

We call this quantity momentum. Momentum is a vector quantity, used to describe motion.

Definition: The linear momentum of a particle is a vector p ⃗ p⃗ defined as 

Momentum

The linear momentum of a particle is a vector p ⃗ \vec{p} defined as

p ⃗ = m v ⃗ \vec{p}=m \vec{v}

Where

p = linear momentum (kg.m.s − 1 )   m = mass (kg) v = velocity (m.s − 1 ) \begin{align}p &= \text{linear momentum (kg.m.s}^{-1}\text{)} \\ m &= \text{mass (kg)} \\ v &= \text{velocity (m.s}^{-1}\text{)} \end{align}


The direction of the linear momentum is the same as the direction of the velocity.


(Note: we will often drop the adjective “linear” as we will only deal with momentum in one dimension)


According to this definition, momentum is related to both the mass and velocity of an object. A small car travelling at the same velocity as a big truck will have a smaller momentum than the truck. A car travelling fast will have a greater momentum than the same car travelling slowly.


Different objects can also have the same momentum, for example a car travelling slowly can have the same momentum as a motor cycle travelling relatively fast.

Calculate the momentum of a car of mass 1000 kg travelling east with a velocity of 8   kg.m.s − 1 \ \text{kg.m.s}^{-1}

p = m v p = ( 1000 ) ( 8 ) p = 8000   kg.m.s − 1   east \begin{align}p &= mv \\ p &= (1000)(8) \\ p &= 8000 \ \text{kg.m.s}^{-1} \ \text{east} \end{align}

Calculate the momentum of a motor cycle of mass 250 kg travelling east at 32 m.s-1.

p = m v = ( 250 ) ( 32 ) = 8000   kg.m.s − 1 \begin{align} p&=mv \\ &=(250)(32) \\ &= 8000 \ \text{kg.m.s}^{-1} \end{align}

Momentum and Newton’s Second Law

Newton’s Second Law of Motion can be defined in terms of momentum.

Newton’s Second Law can be written as

F n e t = m a F_{net}=m a

Where 

F n e t = resultant force (N) m = mass (kg) a = acceleration (m.s − 2 ) \begin{align} F_{net} &= \text{resultant force (N)} \\ m &= \text{mass (kg)} \\ a &= \text{acceleration (m.s}^{-2}\text{)}\end{align} 

But acceleration is the rate of change of velocity and can be written as a = Δ v Δ t a=\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}

Combining these equations 

F n e t = m Δ v Δ t F_{net} =m \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}

So for constant mass

F n e t = Δ p Δ t F_{net} = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}

This is an alternative form of Newton’s second law.

Impulse

When a resultant force acts on an object for a period of time the object will undergo a change in velocity.  Momentum which is the product of mass and velocity must therefore also change.  The change in momentum is directly proportional to the resultant force and to the time for which the force acts.

Starting with Newton's second law,

F n e t = Δ p Δ t F_{net}=\frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}

Rearranging:

F n e t Δ t = Δ p F_{net} \Delta t = \Delta p

The left hand side of this equation is a measure of the strength of the force and the duration for which the force acts. This combination is defined as  impulse. 

Impulse

Definition

The principle of conservation of momentum: The total momentum for a system of 

interacting objects (objects colliding or exploding apart) remains constant,  providing that 

there are no external forces acting on the system.

Note: Before an explosion, the total momentum is zero if nothing is moving.  After an 

explosion, the total momentum is still zero.

When solving problems with momentum use the following formula:

p b e f o r e = p a f t e r m 1 u 1 + m 2 u 2 =   m 1 v 1 + m 2 v 2 \begin{align}p_{before} &= p_{after} \\m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 &= m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2\end{align}

Where

m   = mass k g u   = initial velocity m . s − 1 v   = final velocity m . s − 1 \begin{align}m  &= \text{mass} \left(kg \right) \\ u  &= \text{initial velocity} \left(m.s^{-1}\right) \\ v  &= \text{final velocity} \left(m.s^{-1}\right) \end{align}
